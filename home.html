Who am I?

My name is Julian Krese, and I'm a third-year Computer Science major and Data Science minor at the University of Virginia. Welcome to my personal site! As I navigate the job application process, I realized that my resume can only convey so much about who I am and what defines me. While it outlines my skills, it doesn’t capture the full picture. That's why I created this website—to showcase some basic web development skills and to provide a more personal introduction. The site itself is built using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, with a bit of Bootstrap for features like the carousel above.

So, who is Julian Krese beyond a Computer Science student?

If you haven't gathered by from the photos above, I’m an outdoor enthusiast. While I enjoy hiking, traveling, and exploring, fly fishing is my greatest passion. My dad taught my brother and me to fly fish as kids, and we’ve both become avid anglers since. It has taken me to places from Maine to Montana to the Bahamas and I could talk about it for hours. Beyond fly fishing, I love golfing and playing soccer. I played soccer in high school and now play intramural games. As for golf, I’ve been steadily improving my handicap and currently shoot in the mid-to-low 80s. I’m decent but still have lotsss to improve upon.

So how does an outdoorsman find his way into a tech career?

I’ve always loved building things. Growing up, I was constantly crafting—whether it was LEGO sets, duct-tape wallets, or cubes made of twisty-ties I grabbed at the grocery store. My magnum opus was a ping pong ball dispenser crafted from an old cereal box. This love for creating things was always there, but it faded over time as I got older and school became more demanding. Fortunately, a high school Computer Science course rekindled that spark, letting me reconnect with that childhood curiosity—only now, in the digital realm. This course led me to pursue an introductory programming class in college, where I built a game with PyGame. It solidified my decision to dive deeper into Computer Science, and now I'm eager to turn my passion for coding into a career.

I also believe my outdoorsey-ness isn't in conflict with the tech world and my love for creating. Fly fishing involves lots of creating itself and overlaps with many areas of computer science. Fly tying (creating 'lures' for the fishing itself) involves meticulously crafting perfect replicas of bugs that are often a half the width of a penny. It has taught me the importance of patience, persistence, and attention to detail. Whether finding the right fly pattern to match the hatch or hiking for hours to reach the perfect spot, it's always a demanding sport. These same skills translate well into software engineering, where debugging a complex issue or refining a piece of code requires a similar level of focus and adaptability. Just as I enjoy the challenges and complexity of fly fishing, I also take on the challenges of solving complex problems in programming.
